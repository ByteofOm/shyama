# Language Hygiene Guide

This guide governs the words Shyama uses — and avoids — everywhere: in the app, in marketing, in support responses, and in any public communication.

## Words We USE

- "possible matches"
- "this may be"
- "commonly looks like"
- "skin tips"
- "common conditions that look like this"
- "consider consulting a dermatologist"
- "educational"
- "learn more about"
- "similar in appearance to"
- "this could be"
- "a possible match is"

## Words We NEVER Use

| Word / Phrase | Why |
|---------------|-----|
| diagnose / diagnosis | Implies medical authority we do not have |
| treat / treatment | Implies a therapeutic claim |
| cure | False certainty |
| definitely / certainly | False certainty about a clinical matter |
| "you have [condition]" | Direct attribution of a medical condition |
| "the app detected" | Implies clinical-grade detection |
| Any condition name *without* "possible" / "may be" framing | Makes a definitive clinical claim |

## Why This Matters

Using diagnostic language — "you have X" or "this is definitely Y" — puts users at risk of acting on AI output as medical fact. It can also cause genuine harm when the model is wrong, which it will sometimes be, especially across the full range of skin tones.

Framing also determines regulatory exposure. Diagnostic language places Shyama in FDA Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) territory, which triggers requirements we are not equipped to meet.

**The goal:** empower users to have better, more informed conversations with their dermatologists — not to replace those conversations.

When in doubt, add "possible," "may be," or "consider consulting a professional." If the sentence still sounds authoritative after adding that framing, revise it.
